# Chapter 3 — The Investment

The billing page told the story more honestly than any pitch deck could.

January 7, 2026: CA$31.64. The Anthropic Pro plan, Month 1. Aaron had signed up the way most people sign up for things — curious, noncommittal, half-expecting to cancel after the free trial. He used Claude the way a cautious person test-drives a car: short trips, familiar roads, one hand on the door handle. He asked it questions. He tested its limits. He was exploring.

February 7, 2026: CA$31.64. Pro plan, Month 2. The renewal was automatic, which meant he had not cancelled, which meant something had shifted. He was no longer testing — he was learning. The conversations had gotten longer, more specific, more ambitious. He had started thinking in terms of what Claude could build, not just what it could answer.

March 23, 2026: CA$142.80. The Max plan. This was not an automatic renewal. This was a deliberate decision — a four-and-a-half-times increase in monthly spend, made by a man with no income, twin daughters, and a mortgage in a small Ontario city. He upgraded because he had seen something in the previous two months that most people had not seen yet: this was not a chatbot. This was an execution engine. And he had twenty years of product instinct ready to feed into it.

Two days later, the 72-hour sprint began.

By March 28, Aaron had shipped seven digital products. The Digital Confidence Centre — 241 pages, 21 modules, bilingual, WCAG 2.1 AA accessible. Career Coach. Clarity. Kevin's Apartment Search. Quality Dashboard. The Aaron Patzalek consulting site. The Two Birds Innovation company site. All static HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. All deployed to GitHub Pages. All functional.

Total AI spend from January to March: CA$269.

The developer equivalent of that output — if Aaron had hired a freelance team to build what he built — would have cost somewhere between $30,000 and $60,000. And it would have taken months, not days.

The math was not the point. The point was what it proved: that a non-developer with deep product instinct and the right AI tools could ship production-quality software at a pace that made traditional development economics irrelevant.

He is not a developer. He never was. He is a product manager who found a lever long enough to move the world from his kitchen table.
