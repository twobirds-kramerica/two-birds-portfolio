# Logo v1.2 — Designer Recommendation

## Top 3 Ranked

### #1 Recommendation: V04 — Fraternal Stroke Weights

**Why:** This is the variation where the brand story is most legible at every size. The weight difference (56px vs 42px) is visible at favicon scale and striking at billboard scale. It doesn't rely on color temperature, circle sizes, or transparency tricks that may not survive format conversion. The fraternal distinction — one bold, one lighter — maps perfectly to "two birds, different but related." The heavier top chevron feels like it's leading; the lighter bottom feels like it's following its own path. That's the twin daughters story in two strokes.

**All-size performance:** Excellent. The weight difference reads clearly from 16px to poster size. No fine details that collapse at small sizes.

**Print:** Clean. Pure white on solid blue. No gradients, no transparency, no textures to worry about in CMYK.

**CMO pick for a tech company?** Yes. It's confident without being aggressive. Professional without being generic. The weight difference adds character that a pure symmetric logo lacks. A CMO would recognise that this logo has a story behind it — that's a conversation starter, which is what logos are for.

---

### #2 Recommendation: V01 — Wide Spread, Bold

**Why:** If V04 is too subtle for Aaron's taste (the weight difference requires a second look), V01 is the safe bet. It fixes every problem in v1.1 — spacing, balance, canvas usage — without introducing any new risk. The open-end circles provide fraternal differentiation. Bold 56px strokes command respect. This is the logo that will never embarrass you.

**All-size performance:** Very good. The circles at arm endpoints may merge with the stroke caps at favicon size, but the overall form reads cleanly.

**Print:** Perfect. Solid colours, no effects.

**CMO pick?** Solid but safe. A CMO would approve it without excitement. It looks like a good logo, not a memorable one.

---

### #3 Recommendation: V08 — Asymmetric Angles

**Why:** This is the variation with the most personality. The tight top chevron and wide bottom chevron tell a story of two genuinely different individuals — not just "same shape, different weight." It's the most honest expression of fraternal (not identical) twins. The composition is deliberately imbalanced, which creates visual tension that draws the eye. A viewer's brain tries to reconcile the two shapes and remembers the logo because of it.

**All-size performance:** Good. The shape difference reads even at small sizes. The tight top chevron might feel like a line at favicon scale, though.

**Print:** Clean. No effects or gradients.

**CMO pick?** A bold CMO would love this. A conservative CMO would worry it looks unbalanced. It depends on whether Aaron wants to project "polished tech company" or "founder-led innovation studio." For Two Birds Innovation specifically, the asymmetry feels right — it's a company built on different perspectives.

---

## Which One Best Tells the Twin Daughters Story?

**V10** (big + small) tells the most emotional version — the protective parent-and-child reading is immediate. But it sacrifices the "two equals" framing. If the story is "my daughters inspired the name," V10 wins. If the story is "two different perspectives, equal in value," V04 or V08 wins.

## Which One Works Best at ALL Sizes?

**V04.** The stroke weight difference is the only fraternal distinction that survives every format: favicon, social avatar, business card, billboard, embroidered polo shirt, browser tab. Circles can merge with strokes. Colors can shift in CMYK. Transparency fails in print. But thick vs thin always reads.

## Which Variations to Skip

- **V05** (cosmos): The texture doesn't print and adds complexity without clarity. Drop it.
- **V09** (overlapping): Too complex for a logo. Works as a hero graphic, not a brand mark.
- **V10** (big + small): Powerful storytelling but breaks at favicon size. Save it for marketing materials, not the primary logo.
- **V07** (gradient): Gradients are a print hazard and add no narrative value. The circle size difference is better expressed in V03.

## Final Word

V04 is the recommendation because it's the variation that works hardest with the least effort. One design decision — different stroke weights — carries the entire brand story, survives every format, and gives Aaron something to talk about when someone asks "what's your logo about?" That's what a good logo does: it starts a conversation.
