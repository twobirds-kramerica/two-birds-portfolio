## Row-by-Row Verdicts

### 1. Online kindness counts — words stick longer online than in person
**Category:** Emotional-Safety | **ID:** 35da09cf-876a-813a-9525-f92013de9ab3
**Vera:** Clear. No privacy exposure. Research citations (Valkenburg & Peter 2009, PREVNet Canada) are authoritative.
**Frank:** High deployability. No materials needed beyond discussion prompts. Library and school programme ready with zero prep.
**Dr. Lena annotation:** For children who have been bullied online, the "words stick longer" framing may trigger distress. Include a facilitator note: if a child becomes upset, the response is not "be careful what you say" — it's "tell a trusted adult and we'll figure it out together." The lesson should feel empowering, not alarming.
**Verdict: APPROVED — 1 annotation (build-spec facilitator note).**

### 2. Building a safe online identity — what to share, what to protect
**Category:** Tech-Safety | **ID:** 35ca09cf-876a-812c-8632-e447a6c9f591
**Three annotations:**
1. **Vera:** At age 7-9, many children already have accounts on Roblox, gaming platforms, and YouTube despite platform age limits. The module must acknowledge this reality: "if you have any apps or games where people can see your profile..." is more honest than "when you someday have an account." Also: the "creator luring: strangers posing as peers" dimension requires careful framing — create awareness of the pattern without creating paranoia about all online interactions.
2. **Dr. Lena:** Context-dependency is the 7-9 lesson that wasn't needed at 4-6. Full name alone is different from full name + school + photo together. The module should include a "same information, different contexts" worked example so children understand that sharing rules are about combinations, not individual pieces.
3. **Frank:** Library programmes will encounter a mix of children who have accounts and those who don't. The activity needs a variant prompt for both: "If you have an account on any app or game..." and "When you do have one..." so no child feels excluded from the exercise.
**Verdict: APPROVED — 3 annotations.**

### 3. Watching to learn vs. watching to pass time — four moves
**Category:** Learning | **ID:** 356a09cf-876a-813c-a5c3-e5b3afff8bf9
**Vera:** Clear. No privacy concerns.
**Two annotations:**
1. **Dr. Lena:** At 7-9, metacognitive awareness (thinking about your own watching) is just beginning to form. The framing must be concrete and action-based, not abstract. Use "watching WITH a job to do" vs "watching to relax" as the two primary categories. The four moves should be specific action steps within the "watching to learn" mode — e.g., (1) say out loud what you want to learn, (2) pause and check if you learned it, (3) try the thing you watched, (4) tell someone else what you learned. Avoid the word "purposeful" — too abstract for this age group.
2. **Frank:** The four moves framework needs to be visually present during the activity — not just spoken. Print or display it as a simple numbered list. Discussion-only versions don't stick in group settings for this age.
**Verdict: APPROVED — 2 annotations.**

### 4. Real, made-up, or somewhere in between — sorting what I find online
**Category:** Critical-Thinking | **ID:** 356a09cf-876a-810b-8ab3-e56ee6056444
**Three annotations:**
1. **Vera:** This row involves children sorting online content, which implies active internet use. Any instruction to search or navigate must include explicit adult supervision: "with a grown-up's help, look at..." — identical requirement to the 4-6 Row 6 extension. Never direct a 7-9 year old to navigate the web independently in a DCC module.
2. **Dr. Lena:** The three-bucket framework (verified/opinion/invented) is well-matched to Piaget's concrete operational stage. One word swap: "invented" is better than "made-up" for 7-9 — it sounds neutral rather than dismissive. Also include a worked example of the opinion bucket specifically: "dogs are better than cats" vs "dogs are the most popular pet in Canada" — 7-9 children reliably conflate strong opinions with facts without this distinction.
3. **Frank:** For children with limited home internet access, the "find online examples" element needs a pre-printed fallback: 6-8 sample headlines/statements ready to sort on paper. Essential for library programmes where internet may be unavailable or restricted for participants.
**Verdict: APPROVED — 3 annotations.**

### 5. Pause and show a grown-up whenever an app asks for something
**Category:** Tech-Safety | **ID:** 349a09cf-876a-816f-b441-c4bfce802a0b
**Three annotations:**
1. **Vera:** This builds on 4-6 Row 1r ("pause before tapping"). At 7-9, children operate more independently on devices. The framing must shift from full deferral to growing agency: "I look at what the app is asking, then decide if it seems right, then ask an adult if I'm unsure" — not just "show a grown-up." Full deferral is still correct for high-stakes requests (location, contacts, camera). Low-stakes requests (notification permissions) can begin to be evaluated independently.
2. **Dr. Lena:** The developmental shift from age 4-6 to 7-9 is from "always show a grown-up" to "pause → read → ask yourself if it makes sense → ask an adult if unsure." Four concrete steps, not just a reflex. This teaches decision-making, not just deference — which is appropriate for children who are beginning to use devices semi-independently.
3. **Frank:** The activity becomes concrete with real app permission dialog screenshots as examples. Print 2-3 screenshots of common permission requests (location, contacts, photos) and have children sort them: "Makes sense for this app / Not sure / Seems wrong." Physical printed materials make this tactile and discussable in a group setting.
**Verdict: APPROVED — 3 annotations. Note: significant framing upgrade from the 4-6 version — this is not the same skill, it is the next developmental stage of the same skill.**

### 6. Making something useful for someone else
**Category:** Creative-Making | **ID:** 349a09cf-876a-8192-af6c-d7ec8f7e578b
**Vera:** Clear. Community-oriented making has no privacy exposure or data collection concerns.
**Two annotations:**
1. **Dr. Lena:** Kohlberg Stage 3 (interpersonal relationships, being helpful) makes "useful for someone else" deeply motivating at age 7-9. One annotation: clarify that "useful" includes emotionally useful — a kind note, a birthday card, a drawing for a friend — not only practically useful (a how-to guide, a tool). Without this, children may disqualify their most natural creative outputs.
2. **Frank:** Add a display or sharing option: "with your caregiver's permission, you can give or show what you made to the person you made it for." The social completion of delivering the useful thing reinforces the lesson. Library programmes can make this a group activity: "make something useful for another person in this room."
**Verdict: APPROVED — 2 annotations.**

### 7. Telling a grown-up when something online feels weird
**Category:** Emotional-Safety | **ID:** 349a09cf-876a-81c0-9d73-c5997f72a204
**Three annotations:**
1. **Vera:** Platform-level annotation: this module's effectiveness depends on children having a named safe grown-up from the 4-6 "Who is my safe grown-up?" module. Reference that module explicitly in the activity: "Remember your safe grown-up list? This is exactly when you use it." Creates curriculum continuity and reinforces the prior skill.
2. **Dr. Lena:** "Feels weird" is exactly right for 7-9 — somatic and emotional cues are more reliable disclosure triggers than abstract rules at this age. One annotation: include concrete examples of what "feels weird" might physically feel like — "your stomach feels tight," "you want to close the screen fast," "something seems wrong but you can't say exactly why." These physical signal descriptions help children identify the feeling before it passes and before they talk themselves out of disclosing.
3. **Frank:** Critical annotation for library programmes: staff running this module should be prepared to receive a disclosure. Add a brief facilitator note: "If a child tells you something online felt weird, take it seriously, respond calmly, and follow your organisation's safeguarding protocol. Your library or school will have one." Library facilitators need this note — they are not trained counsellors and should know the expected next step.
**Verdict: APPROVED — 3 annotations.**
