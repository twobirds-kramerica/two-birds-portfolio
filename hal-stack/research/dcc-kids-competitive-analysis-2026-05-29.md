## 2. What Each Competitor Covers

**MediaSmarts** is Canada's anchor institution for this space. Their "Use, Understand & Engage" framework covers: privacy/security, finding and verifying, consumer awareness (including online marketing to kids, "Pay for Play" games), community engagement, and creative production. Framework grades run K–3, 4–6, 7–8, and 9–12. "Break the Fake" (grades 3–12) addresses misinformation. The "Online Marketing to Kids" unit (grades 6–9) touches on persuasive platform design — the closest existing Canadian resource to DCC's proposed engagement-bait topic. MediaSmarts does NOT have a device-specific track, family co-use model, or standalone home-use format.

**Digital Moment** focuses on coding, AI literacy (new 2025 framework), and algorithm/data literacy. Strong educator orientation. Not a digital safety or media literacy programme per se — it is a digital skills programme. Offers free classroom-ready lessons for K–12 aligned to Ontario and Quebec. No family/home model.

**Common Sense Education** offers the most comprehensive free K–12 curriculum globally. Covers cyberbullying, privacy, creative credit, news literacy, screen time, and advertising/persuasion. Their "Don't Take the Bait" lesson addresses how apps create "curiosity gaps" — the closest US equivalent to the engagement-bait topic. Age-grade spiral structure is strong. No Canadian curriculum alignment. No offline/library facilitation model.

**Google Be Internet Awesome / Interland** targets 7–12 with a gamified approach: safety, privacy, phishing, kindness, and fact-checking. No persuasion/engagement-bait content. Self-directed browser game — no facilitator structure, no family co-use, no age-cohort progression.

**iKeepSafe** is US-based, ISTE/CASEL-aligned. Strong for schools; no Canadian context. Covers online safety, privacy, and digital balance. No engagement-bait or platform-manipulation content.

**CanCode** is a federal grant programme, not a direct-delivery curriculum. It funds orgs like Digital Moment. Relevant as a funding/partnership signal, not a competitor.

**Algorithm & Data Literacy Project** targets older students (10+) on how recommendation systems and data collection work. Closest to platform-mechanics education but aimed at secondary level, not ages 7–9.

**News Literacy Project** addresses engagement bait explicitly as a concept in their educator materials — but at grades 6–12, in a US context, within an LMS (Checkology). No Canadian delivery, no younger-child version.

## 4. Gap Analysis: "Engagement Bait / Platform Manipulation" for Ages 7–9

**Question:** Does any competitor cover YouTube "Would You Rather"-style engagement farming, explained to young children (ages 7–9)?

**Finding: No. This is a genuine content gap.**

- MediaSmarts' closest lesson ("Online Marketing to Kids") targets grades 6–9 — too old.
- Common Sense Education's "Don't Take the Bait" covers curiosity gaps but is framed for grades 6–8 (ages 11–13) and focuses on news/misinformation, not video platform mechanics.
- The News Literacy Project defines engagement bait as a concept but only for grades 6–12 and in a US context.
- Google Interland covers phishing and safety — no attention-economy or recommendation-loop content at all.
- Research confirms the problem is real and age-specific: a Michigan Medicine / JAMA Network Open study found children are frequently exposed to problematic clickbait during YouTube searches, and developmentally, ages 7–9 have greater impulsivity and less capacity to recognise persuasion tactics.

**DCC opportunity:** A module specifically explaining to 7–9 year olds *why* YouTube shows "Would You Rather" videos, how "Would you rather eat 1,000 worms OR never eat pizza again?" is designed to make them comment (engagement farming), and what the creator/platform gains — would be the only resource of its kind in Canada and likely the English-speaking world aimed at this age group in a home/library-facilitated format.

## 5. Regulatory and Government Landscape (Canada)

- **Ontario Language Curriculum (2024 revision):** Grades 1–8 include a "Digital Media Literacy" strand (strand A2) with explicit outcomes. This is the strongest curriculum hook for DCC Kids in the largest Canadian province. MediaSmarts has funded resources mapped to this strand — DCC Kids should map its modules to the same outcomes.
- **MediaSmarts:** Receives federal and provincial government funding; is effectively the government's designated agency for school-facing digital literacy. DCC should position as *complementary* (family/home/library) rather than competitive.
- **CRTC:** Acknowledges digital literacy importance but explicitly states other parties are better placed to deliver it. CRTC's role is broadcast/platform regulation, not curriculum. Not a direct partner or blocker.
- **Bill C-63 (Online Harms Act):** Being revived in the 2026 parliamentary session. Would establish Canada's first statutory framework for harmful online content. Creates regulatory tailwind for any programme addressing child online safety — DCC Kids is well-positioned as a grassroots response.
- **Bill S-209:** Targets age assurance on pornographic platforms — narrow scope, not directly relevant to DCC.
- **CanCode ($39.2M renewed, 2026):** Funds K–12 digital skills orgs. Potential future funding avenue for DCC's library/school facilitation track.
- **Privacy Commissioner (October 2025 joint resolution):** All provincial privacy commissioners issued a joint call for stronger protection of children's privacy in educational technologies. Reinforces DCC's no-login, no-data-collection static model as a governance advantage.

---

## 6. Positioning Summary

DCC Kids occupies a white space: **the only free, static-web, Canadian, family-and-library-facilitated digital literacy programme for children ages 4–15 with age-cohort tracks, device-specific content, and planned engagement-bait/platform-manipulation modules for the 7–9 age group.**

MediaSmarts is the government-adjacent incumbent — position DCC as its home-use complement, not its replacement. Common Sense is the international heavyweight for schools. DCC's structural advantages (no login, no app, works offline via browser cache, library-printable facilitation pack) give it access to demographics none of these reach: low-income families, rural library patrons, and caregivers who distrust data-collecting platforms.
