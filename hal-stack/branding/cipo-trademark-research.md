## 2. Registration Process — Step by Step

1. **Trademark search** — Search CIPO database + broader common-law search for unregistered marks
2. **Determine Nice classes** — Identify which classes cover your goods/services
3. **Prepare application** — Trademark representation, goods/services descriptions, applicant details
4. **File online with CIPO** — Submit at https://ised-isde.canada.ca and receive filing number
5. **CIPO examination** — Government reviews for conflicts and legal compliance (6-12 months)
6. **Journal publication** — Mark published in Trademarks Journal for 2-month opposition period
7. **Registration** — Receive certificate confirming exclusive rights across Canada

## 5. DIY vs. Trademark Agent

### DIY Pros
- Cost: ~$491-$789 (government fees only)
- Full control over timing
- Adequate for straightforward marks in uncrowded classes

### DIY Risks
- Missed conflicts (phonetic similarities, design marks, unregistered common-law marks)
- Poorly drafted goods/services descriptions → narrower protection
- Filing fees non-refundable if application refused
- No professional to handle examiner objections

### Agent Pros
- Comprehensive clearance search reduces refusal risk
- Properly drafted descriptions maximize protection scope
- Professional handles objections and opposition
- Registered trademarks increase business valuation by ~11.9% (per Heer Law research)

### Agent Cons
- $2,000-$4,000 minimum cost
- Still takes 12-18 months regardless

### Verdict for Aaron's Situation
**DIY is viable** for a first filing if the CIPO search comes back clean. The "Two Birds" name is descriptive enough that conflicts may exist, but Aaron's specific combination "Two Birds Innovation" in technology/consulting classes may be distinct enough. If the CIPO search reveals existing "Two Birds" marks in Class 35 or 42, hiring an agent for a proper clearance opinion ($500-$1,000) before filing would be the smart move.
