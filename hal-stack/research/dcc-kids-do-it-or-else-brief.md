## The Idea (Aaron's words, reconstructed)

### The Spectrum Aaron Described

**Level 1 — Mild peer coercion (ages 5–7, on the bus, playground):**
- "Do it or I won't be your friend"
- "Do it or I'm gonna tell someone you're a poo poo head"
- "Do it or you're not smart"
- Small, slight, easily dismissed — but the pattern starts here

**Level 2 — Escalating peer pressure (ages 8–12):**
- More deliberate, more potentially harmful
- Still peer-to-peer but stakes feel higher to the child
- Can move into digital: dare chains, "send me this or I'll tell everyone," group pressure in chats

**Level 3 — Digital adult bad actors (ages 10+, but introduce concepts earlier):**
- "Do this or I'll hurt your dog"
- "Do this or I'll tell your mom"
- "You'll get in trouble if you tell anyone"
- "Santa won't come" (younger children)
- "No one will believe you"
- This is the grooming-adjacent, deliberate coercion territory

**Level 4 — Even trusted adults:**
- Aaron explicitly included: "even if it's at their own parent, potentially"
- This is child abuse recognition territory — coercive language from authority figures
- Requires careful, age-appropriate framing without creating blanket distrust of adults
- The "safe grown-up" network concept is the protective anchor here

## The Key Pedagogical Distinction — Surprises vs Secrets

This is the foundational child-safety framework for this age group:

| Surprises | Secrets |
|-----------|---------|
| Have an end date ("tell him Saturday!") | No end date ("never tell anyone") |
| Feel exciting, warm | Feel heavy, shameful, scary |
| You choose to keep them | You feel forced to keep them |
| Telling would spoil something nice | You're told bad things will happen if you tell |
| Shared openly after reveal | Hidden forever |

**The rule:** Surprises are okay. Secrets that feel scary or heavy — ALWAYS tell a safe grown-up.

## What Kids Should DO (Aaron's explicit ask)

1. **Name it:** "That's a threat, not a choice."
2. **Not comply:** A real friend doesn't threaten you. A real adult doesn't threaten children.
3. **Tell a safe grown-up immediately** — even if the threat is "don't tell."
4. **Understand the threat is a manipulation tool** — it's designed to make you feel afraid to tell. That's exactly why you should tell.
5. **Digital-specific:** Screenshot and tell an adult. Don't delete the message.

## Proposed Cohort-Specific Structure

### Ages 4–6: "That's Not How Friends Work"
- Focus: peer coercion ("I won't be your friend unless...")
- The "surprises vs secrets" framework in age-appropriate language
- Safe grown-up network (already started in existing DCC 4-6 content)
- Simple rule: "If it makes your tummy feel yucky, tell a safe grown-up."
- No bad actors — just peer dynamics at this age

### Ages 7–9: "When Someone Tries to Scare You Into Doing Something"
- Peer pressure escalation + introduction of online threats
- "Do it or I'll tell everyone" — group chats, online dares
- What coercion looks like vs normal requests
- The threat is the sign something is wrong — safe grown-ups need to know
- Practice: scenario cards (printable, no device needed)

### Ages 10–12: "Do It or Else — Recognising Manipulation Online and In Person"
- Full spectrum: peer, digital stranger, adult bad actor
- Grooming language patterns: "this is our secret," "no one will believe you," "you'll get in trouble"
- Distinction: bad actors always try to isolate (separate you from your safe people)
- Digital evidence: screenshot, don't delete
- Explicit: even if the person threatening you is an adult you know, it is still wrong and still needs to be told to another safe adult
- Escalation path: safe grown-up → school counsellor → Kids Help Phone (1-800-668-6868)

## Research to Incorporate (Aaron asked for real academic backing)

### Relevant frameworks and bodies of literature:
- **Darkness to Light / Stewards of Children** — coercion as a grooming tool; the secrecy mandate
- **Finkelhor (1984, 2008)** — child sexual abuse models; grooming stages; secrecy as a precondition
- **Boulton & Underwood (1992)** — peer victimisation in children; social exclusion threats
- **Kohlberg moral development** — Stage 1 (ages 5-7): punishment-and-obedience orientation — why young children comply with "you'll get in trouble"
- **Erikson** — industry vs inferiority (ages 7-12): social belonging is existential — "I won't be your friend" is developmentally devastating at this age
- **Kids Help Phone Canada** — Canadian-specific reporting pathway; 1-800-668-6868; text HELLO to 686868
- **Cybertip.ca** — Canadian reporting for online exploitation
- **Lanning (2010), FBI** — grooming behaviour taxonomy; secrecy and coercion as core mechanisms
- **Media Smarts Canada** — digital coercion framing for Canadian context

### Canadian regulatory/legal context:
- **Criminal Code of Canada s.172.1** — luring a child via internet; relevant for adult bad actor framing
- **CCCP (Canadian Centre for Child Protection)** — resources and reporting
- **Ontario Child Protection Act** — mandatory reporting; children need to know adults are required to help
