## Row-by-Row Verdicts

### 1. Spotting hidden advertising — when someone you like is paid to say so
**Category:** Critical-Thinking | **ID:** 35ca09cf-876a-8142-ba37-fc1991f4e98e
**Vera:** Ad disclosure is fundamentally a consent/transparency issue — children have a right to know when commercial persuasion is happening. Canadian Ad Standards disclosure requirements apply. No privacy data-collection risk from this module itself, but annotation: the module should acknowledge that behavioural targeting is how children become the subject of personalised advertising; #ad/#sponsored signals only address the visible layer.
**Frank:** Ad Detective Challenge gamification is a good library-programme format — pairs or small groups, printed screenshots of disclosed vs. undisclosed sponsored posts work well because many libraries restrict YouTube. Essential: facilitator must have 6–8 printed examples ready. Two-person "detective and witness" structure keeps the discussion alive.
**Dr. Lena annotation:** Parasocial relationships (Horton & Wohl 1956) make influencer ads more persuasive, not less — children trust a creator's recommendation precisely because they feel they know them. The module must name this directly: "When you really like someone online, ads they make feel more real than ones from brands you don't know. That's why companies pay them." Without this, children dismiss the lesson as applying to influencers they don't personally follow. Also: avoid framing all influencer content as deceptive — most creators who disclose are behaving correctly; the lesson is spotting the ones who don't.
**Verdict: APPROVED — 3 annotations (build-spec: parasocial framing + printed examples + disclosure-layer scope note).**

### 2. Spotting a 'please don't tell your parent' message
**Category:** Emotional-Safety | **ID:** 348a09cf-876a-81b6-896e-d9ab0a9f234a
**Vera:** The secrecy-request pattern aligns with predatory behaviour definitions under the Canadian Centre for Child Protection (Cybertip.ca). Auto-delete platforms (Snapchat, Telegram) are specifically used by bad actors because evidence disappears — the module should name this. One annotation: avoid platform-shaming (children use Snapchat for legitimate peer communication); the lesson is about secrecy requests on ANY platform, not the platform itself. No privacy-data concern from the module.
**Dr. Lena:** Critical annotation: at 10-12, peer secrets are developmentally normal and emotionally meaningful — this is what makes the grooming "don't tell" request effective. The module MUST distinguish clearly: "Trusted adults — parents, teachers, coaches — support you in telling other trusted adults. Someone trying to harm you asks you to keep secrets FROM your parents." Children must hear this distinction explicitly or the lesson will not transfer to real situations. Second annotation: children at this age often feel shame or self-blame when targeted. Framing must be "this is a pattern adults use to trick kids; it is not about anything you did." Shame prevents disclosure — the module's tone must remove it.
**Frank:** No gamification is the right call — the reward is the relationship and the response practice. Muscle-memory script ("I'm telling [safe adult]. This feels off.") is the right output. Library facilitator note is mandatory: facilitators running this module must know their organisation's safeguarding/disclosure protocol before the session. Add explicitly: "If a child tells you something during this activity, take it seriously, respond calmly, and follow your organisation's child safety protocol. Do not promise confidentiality."
**Verdict: APPROVED — 4 annotations. Highest-sensitivity module in the 10-12 cohort. Build with facilitator note as non-negotiable component.**

### 3. Following a story back to where it started — SIFT source tracing
**Category:** Critical-Thinking | **ID:** 355a09cf-876a-8171-b12f-cbfc12a8568b
**Vera:** No privacy concerns. Strong alignment with MediaSmarts Canada and News Literacy Project resources — both are credible Canadian sources. The "leave the page" habit (Caulfield 2019) is the most counter-intuitive element and the most important one to teach: children have been trained by platform design to stay engaged, not to leave and check. This is worth calling out explicitly.
**Dr. Lena:** 10-12 is the right developmental window for SIFT — formal operational thinking is beginning to emerge (Piaget). Children this age can hold two representations of a claim simultaneously: "what this says" and "what the evidence actually shows." Key annotation: children need to experience SIFT working before they trust it. The first activity must use a pre-selected debunked viral claim with a known fact-check trail — asking children to find their own example the first time risks either a clean claim (lesson doesn't land) or a traumatising topic (harm). Pre-select a safe, shareable example (health myth, wildlife fact, sports record).
**Frank:** The 60-second source trace is an excellent library-programme format — works well as a timed group activity. One annotation: library computers often filter fact-check aggregators (Snopes, PolitiFact) via content controls. Pre-download or print the worked example's fact-check trail so the activity is not blocked mid-session. MediaSmarts Canada resources are typically available on library systems.
**Verdict: APPROVED — 3 annotations (pre-selected claim required; filter-bypass prep for libraries; "leave the page" framing must be explicit).**

### 4. Making a story with AI as my helper, not my author
**Category:** Creative-Making | **ID:** 348a09cf-876a-81bd-a6e9-cbbf1edbcd39
**Vera:** Critical annotation: major AI chat tools (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini) have minimum age requirements of 13. The module must not direct 10-12-year-old children to create their own accounts. Correct framing: the activity uses a parent/caregiver's account in a supervised session, OR uses a pre-printed set of AI-generated sentence options so no login is required at all. The "real AI tools don't ask kids personal questions about home/school" safety annotation must remain — it is the most important child-safety signal in this row.
**Dr. Lena:** The "My Line / AI Line" framework (7/10 minimum children's lines) is well-calibrated for 10-12 (Erikson: industry — children want to produce real work, not have it done for them). Annotation: include a discussion of "AI-assisted vs AI-generated" — children must be able to explain what they contributed, both for their own creative identity and because teachers will ask. The story-ownership meter (1–10) is an excellent reflective tool; make it physical (sticker on a printed scale) for group settings.
**Frank:** For library programmes, the no-login variant (pre-printed AI sentence options) is the practical choice — avoids account management, parental consent logistics, and network restrictions. Facilitator prepares 3 sets of AI options for 3 common story prompts; children choose their prompt and start from Line 1. The activity is fully self-contained without any live AI access.
**Verdict: APPROVED — 3 annotations (account age restriction must be addressed; AI-assisted vs AI-generated discussion required; no-login library variant needed).**

### 5. Using a password manager
**Category:** Tech-Safety | **ID:** 349a09cf-876a-811d-b763-c94c8fdef76e
**Vera:** Strong alignment with CISA and NIST SP 800-63B. Bitwarden (open source, audited) and 1Password (strong privacy record) are both appropriate recommendations. Annotation: the module must be framed as a FAMILY SETUP activity — not something a child does alone. The family plan structure requires a parent/caregiver as account owner. Children at 10-12 should have their own vault items, but the account is a family account. PIPEDA: both tools have compliant privacy policies; Bitwarden is open-source and self-hostable if a family has higher privacy requirements.
**Dr. Lena:** ONE-TIME SETUP is the right framing. At 10-12, children can participate meaningfully in setup — they can help choose the master passphrase and add their own accounts. Annotation: after setup, the child should have at least one account they manage themselves in the vault — the skill-transfer is the goal, not just watching a parent set it up. The Diceware master passphrase method is appropriate for this age and memorable.
**Frank:** 60–90 minutes is too long for a library programme session. The library version must be a "preview and prepare" session (20 minutes): show how it works, explain why, send home with a printed setup checklist. Add explicit facilitator note: "This is a homework task that families complete together, not a library session task. The session's goal is to motivate and prepare, not to complete setup." fr-QC: currently empty — flag for bilingual delivery.
**Verdict: APPROVED — 3 annotations (family-account framing required; library version = preview-only; fr-QC content gap). NOTE: prerequisite for this module: child has completed "Creating a strong password" — flag build dependency.**

### 6. When I remix something, I name who made the original
**Category:** Creative-Making | **ID:** 349a09cf-876a-8124-a6ff-cdda22772fd9
**Vera:** Creative Commons and copyright attribution are directly relevant to digital citizenship. Annotation: Canadian Copyright Act should be cited alongside Creative Commons — Crown copyright in Canada (government materials) has different rules from CC and from US fair use. Children making memes with government images or school materials may encounter Crown copyright; a brief note prevents a common misconception. PIPEDA has no direct application here.
**Dr. Lena:** Kohlberg Stage 3 (interpersonal ethics — doing right by the people in your community) is the correct motivational frame for 10-12. Fan art, memes, Roblox mods, TikTok stitches are perfect examples because children are already creating in these formats. Key annotation: avoid legalistic framing ("you could get in trouble if you don't credit"). The better motivator at this stage is community ethics: "Creators appreciate being credited. It's how creative communities work. When you credit someone, you're being the kind of person other creators want to collaborate with."
**Frank:** "Sources named running count" gamification works well for library programmes — a physical tally card children mark as they add credits. fr-QC content is currently empty: before the French build goes live, the fr-QC version needs attribution examples using Québécois creators and platforms (Les 7 Doigts de la Main, local artists, Peerless or Wattpad fr-CA community). English build can proceed independently.
**Verdict: APPROVED — 3 annotations (Canadian Copyright Act citation; community-ethics framing over legalistic; fr-QC gap documented). English build unblocked.**

### 7. Is this an ad, or did they really mean it?
**Category:** Critical-Thinking | **ID:** 348a09cf-876a-815b-9435-df11c72e1a24
**Age lens:** 10-12 (age-differentiated version: kid leads, caregiver checks in)
**Vera:** At 10-12, influencer marketing through YouTube and TikTok is the dominant ad format children encounter. Annotation: the 10-12 version should address disclosure placement specifically — #ad buried at end of a 3-minute video vs. disclosed in the first 3 seconds is meaningfully different. Children need to develop the habit of looking for disclosure at the start, not assuming it will be obvious.
**Dr. Lena:** At 10-12, parasocial bonds with specific creators are strong. The 10-12 kid-led version should include a worked example involving a creator the child actually follows — cognitive dissonance is part of the learning ("but I trust them"). Facilitating the moment when a child realises a favourite creator did not disclose is more valuable than any abstract rule. Prepare caregivers for this: "It's okay for the child to feel disappointed. That feeling is the lesson working."
**Frank:** The kid-leads structure (10-12) works well in library pairs with a facilitator check-in rather than constant guidance. Two children can watch the same clip and compare notes before the facilitator joins the discussion. Library logistics: use a library-accessible YouTube substitute (pre-downloaded clip or screenshot sequence) to avoid network issues.
**Verdict: APPROVED — 3 annotations (10-12 lens). Multi-age build: 7-9 version = caregiver-paired; 10-12 version = kid-leads; 13-15 version = teaches a sibling or peer.**

### 8. Creating a strong password I can remember
**Category:** Tech-Safety | **ID:** 348a09cf-876a-81ee-a960-fddd07c64331
**Age lens:** 10-12 (age-differentiated: can complete solo with adult check-in)
**Vera:** Strong privacy foundation — reused or weak passwords are a direct PIPEDA/identity risk. Annotation: the 10-12 version should briefly connect to real data breaches (HaveIBeenPwned is appropriate to mention for facilitated, caregiver-supervised contexts). Knowing that millions of passwords from platforms children use have been exposed makes the lesson concrete and urgent without being alarmist.
**Dr. Lena:** At 10-12, children increasingly have their own accounts on gaming platforms, school portals, and social apps — this skill is directly applicable, not hypothetical. Solo completion with adult check-in is developmentally appropriate (growing autonomy + safety net). The passphrase method (MyCat8PurpleSocks!) is memorable and correct for this age. No additional framing concerns.
**Frank:** 20 minutes is right for a library session. At 10-12, the activity is self-directed with the facilitator checking the final passphrase against length and character requirements (not reading the actual passphrase). Annotation: explicitly pair this module with "Using a password manager" — this skill becomes the entry point to that one. Print the dependency clearly in the facilitator notes: "After today, you're ready for the password manager session."
**Verdict: APPROVED — 2 annotations (10-12 lens). Build dependency: prerequisite for Using a password manager.**

### 9. Asking good questions when I search or ask AI
**Category:** Learning | **ID:** 348a09cf-876a-8136-9830-ef44e2835482
**Age lens:** 10-12 (age-differentiated: 20 min with caregiver check-in)
**Vera:** No privacy concerns for the activity itself. Annotation: the "compare Google vs AI answers" element requires a device with AI access. At 10-12, AI tool use must be caregiver-supervised (see Row 4 annotation — age restrictions apply). The comparison activity should use a parent/caregiver's account for the AI side, or use a pre-printed set of AI responses to the same question for the no-login library variant.
**Dr. Lena:** At 10-12, the Three-Why Game should go deeper than at 7-9 — not just refining the question, but examining WHY different sources return different answers. This connects to metacognitive awareness (Flavell 1979): children at this age can begin to reason about the limits of a source, not just the content. One concrete prompt: "What would have to be true about where this source comes from for this answer to be correct?"
**Frank:** 20 minutes with caregiver check-in is right. For library programmes: the "compare Google vs AI answers" element needs a pre-selected neutral topic (e.g., "how do migratory birds navigate?") to avoid controversial content on shared screens. Facilitator pre-runs the comparison and prints both answers so the activity is fully paper-based if needed. Pre-selection prevents mid-session topic drift into sensitive areas.
**Verdict: APPROVED — 3 annotations (10-12 lens; AI access must be caregiver-supervised; pre-selected topic required for library settings).**
