## Post 3 — Friday, April 11 (Trust-First Design)

The worst thing a digital product can do to a nervous user is make them feel stupid.

I learned this building a literacy platform for seniors. My test persona is Brenda — 74 years old, alone in St. Thomas, terrified of her iPad.

Module 1 doesn't teach her how to use the device. Module 1 teaches her how to leave. "If something scary appears on your screen, press this button. You're safe. You can always come back."

We teach the escape hatch before we teach anything else. Fear first. Empowerment second.

This approach — trust-first design — doesn't just work for seniors. It works for any user who didn't choose to be there. Government forms. Healthcare portals. Employee onboarding systems.

If your users are scared, the problem isn't the users. It's the design.

## Post 6 — Friday, April 18 (Trust-First Design 2)

For a 77-year-old woman named Helen, those 47 notifications aren't engagement. They're anxiety. She misses her daughter's message because it's buried under weather alerts and game updates.

## Post 7 — Monday, April 21 (Float-Free Philosophy)

Every tool I use could disappear tomorrow.

GitHub could go down. The AI API could triple its prices. Google could shut off my email. Cloudflare could change their terms.

So I documented every dependency, found a free alternative for each, and wrote a 24-hour escape plan. I call it the Float-Free Architecture. My current independence score is 48 out of 100. I'm aiming for 80 by the end of the month.

This isn't paranoia. It's product management applied to your own business. If you can't survive the loss of any single vendor, you don't own your business — they do.

The tariff situation is making this real for a lot of people right now. What's your independence score?
