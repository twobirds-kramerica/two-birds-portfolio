## The Idea (Aaron's words)

YouTube/tablet "Would You Rather" content targets kids ~6–7+. Format: a creator asks
"Would you rather: like this video OR have poop on your car?" — a silly binary choice
that forces an engagement action (like, comment). The kid thinks it's a fun game.
The creator gets money from the inflated engagement metrics.

Aaron's framing: "The person getting the money should have horns on their head.
Because they did that whole thing just to take money from a child."

Key insight: it's shown as playful, but the other person is getting paid. Kids don't
recognise it as manipulation because it doesn't look like an ad.

---

## Why This Is a Perfect DCC Kids Module

1. **Kids already know the format** — they've seen it, probably done it
2. **Concrete and relatable** — better than abstract "advertisers track your data"
3. **Reveals the hidden commercial layer** — playful surface, profit underneath
4. **Bridges to existing curriculum** — direct extension of "Spotting hidden advertising"
   and "Is this an ad?" activities
5. **The "horns" visual** — makes the bad actor vivid and memorable; the creator
   chose to manipulate a child for money. That's the lesson.

---

## The Manipulation Mechanics (what the module should explain)

| What it looks like to the kid | What it actually is |
|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| A fun "would you rather" game | Engagement bait — like/comment farming |
| Two silly choices | Forced binary action (no "neither" option) |
| The creator being playful | Monetising child interactions |
| "I chose — I win!" | The creator won: your engagement = their ad revenue |

**The business model the kid needs to understand:**
- YouTube pays creators based on views, likes, comments, watch time
- A like or comment from a 7-year-old is worth money to the creator
- The "game" was designed to extract that engagement — not to entertain

---

## The "Horns" Visual Concept

Aaron's instinct: depict the creator with horns. This is pedagogically strong because:
- Makes the moral distinction concrete for young learners
- Parallels fairy-tale/story structures kids already know (villain = horns)
- Not demonising all YouTubers — only those who run engagement bait specifically
- The visual anchor makes the concept sticky and memorable

Consider: a split illustration — "What you see" (fun creator, silly choices) vs
"What's really happening" (creator with horns, money flowing in from kid interactions).
