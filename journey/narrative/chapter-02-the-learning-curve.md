# Chapter 2: The Learning Curve

The first project was a karate website.

Not a consulting engagement. Not a revenue-generating product. A free site for the martial arts school where Aaron's daughters trained — a goodwill gesture that doubled as a proving ground. If he was going to sell AI-augmented product development to paying clients, he needed to know what it actually felt like to ship something with these tools.

It felt, at first, like trying to have a conversation with someone who was simultaneously brilliant and amnesiac. Claude Code could write beautiful HTML in seconds, but it could not remember what it had written ten minutes ago. Aaron would explain a layout, watch it materialise, then ask for a small change and watch the entire page restructure itself in a direction he had not asked for. The context window — the amount of conversation the AI could hold in its head at once — was a hard ceiling that he hit repeatedly.

He learned to work around it. Gmail drafts became his external memory system. At the end of each session, Aaron would dictate a summary of what had been built, what was broken, and what needed to happen next. He would paste it into the next session as a briefing document. It was clumsy. It worked. The friction was the teacher.

The laptop situation did not help. The i5 Lenovo was his primary machine — a six-year-old workhorse with 8 gigabytes of RAM and a habit of running hot under sustained load. When it needed to cool down, Aaron would switch to the Pentium Silver — a machine so underpowered that it felt like coding through treacle. But it kept the momentum alive during the gaps, and momentum was everything.

He worked late. Past midnight, most nights. The kitchen table became the office. The twin daughters would come downstairs for water and find their father still typing, still talking to himself, still saying "just one more thing." The energy was not manic — it was the focused, slightly obsessive flow of someone who had found the right problem at the right time.

The raw session notes from those first days are full of typos, half-finished thoughts, and the kind of blunt honesty that only appears when nobody is watching. "I just want to walk away now and get off this keyboard," reads one message from late on a Tuesday night. He did not walk away. He finished the module, committed the code, and went to bed at 1am.

The learning curve was steep, but it was not the technology that made it hard. The technology was learnable — every developer who has ever picked up a new framework knows that feeling of incompetence giving way to fluency. What made it hard was the emotional weight of building something that mattered with tools that were still being invented. There was no playbook for this. No senior engineer to pair with. No team standup to catch mistakes. Just Aaron, the terminal, and the growing conviction that this strange new way of working — human intent shaped by AI execution — was going to change everything.

The Elite Karate site shipped. It was clean, responsive, and functional. The school owner had not yet decided whether to use it. That did not matter. What mattered was that Aaron had built something from nothing in a single session, and he understood, viscerally, what he was selling.

The learning curve was not an obstacle to be overcome. It was the point. Every friction point, every context-window crash, every midnight debugging session was a data point about how real businesses would experience this technology. Aaron was not just learning to use AI. He was learning to teach other people to trust it.

And that, he was starting to realise, was worth more than the code.
