**Date:** 2026-05-29 | **Scope:** Canadian-first, senior/adult digital literacy

## DCC Differentiators

- **Structured curriculum depth, not one-off workshops.** Most Canadian competitors deliver standalone workshops or generic resource packs. DCC's 29-module course is a sequenced programme — closest to Be Connected Australia in ambition, but without government dependency.
- **Self-serve, always-on, zero bandwidth.** Static HTML/CSS/JS on GitHub Pages works on slow rural connections with no login, no app, no account. No identified competitor delivers a fully offline-capable, zero-infrastructure programme at this depth.
- **Institutional licensing fills a real gap.** No Canadian nonprofit competitor charges institutions a site fee — all are grant-funded or donor-subsidised. Private players charge but are in-person and quote-based. DCC's $500–$2k/location/year sits in the gap between "free but grant-dependent" and "expensive bespoke training."
- **Family layer.** DCC explicitly targets adult children (45–60) of seniors as a co-audience. No identified Canadian competitor names family members as a primary user.
- **Canadian content by default.** Be Connected (AU) and AARP/Senior Planet (US) are the most polished international programmes but are jurisdiction-specific. DCC covers Canadian services (CRA MyAccount, eHealth, etc.) out of the box.

---

## Pricing Validation: Is $500–$2k/Site/Year Credible?

**Yes — with caveats.**

- The Canadian field is almost entirely free-to-institution and grant-funded. Libraries and senior centres have no established habit of paying for digital literacy curriculum licences.
- The DLEP ending (March 2025) removed ~$47M in federal funding over two cycles. Gluu, Connected Canadians, and HelpAge Canada all relied on DLEP. This creates a real opening.
- The New Horizons for Seniors Program (NHSP) is the current federal fallback — grants up to $50k/project for 2026–27 — but requires institutions to apply, not just buy a licence.
- **The $500 floor** is appropriate for small rural libraries. **The $2k ceiling** suits larger institutions (multi-branch library systems, long-term care chains). Tiered model recommended: $500 / $1,000 / $2,000 by size.
- Be Connected Australia validates government willingness to fund community orgs at $2,500–$52,500 per project — DCC's licence cost is well below what governments already treat as reasonable per-site spend.

---

## DLEP Funding Gap

- **DLEP Phase 1:** $29.5M over 2017–2022. **Phase 2:** $17.6M over 2022–2025. Total: ~$47M. Reached 650,000+ Canadians.
- **Program authorities ended March 31, 2025.** No direct successor announced.
- NHSP is project-grant-based (annual application, up to $50k) — does not fill the structural role DLEP played.
- Organisations like Gluu that built their operating model on DLEP are now vulnerable. Libraries that accessed Gluu resources for free have no guaranteed supply line. **DCC's institutional moment is now.**

---

## Red Flags and Blind Spots

- **"Free" is the dominant expectation.** Every well-known Canadian senior digital literacy brand is free. Sales motion needs a clear answer: DCC is structured curriculum + always-on delivery + no grant dependency. Connected Canadians requires volunteers and scheduling.
- **Volunteer-taught programs dominate.** HelpAge Canada, Connected Canadians, and Gluu all rely on volunteer trainers — low-cost but inconsistent. DCC's self-serve model avoids this but has no human touch. Consider a "facilitator guide" add-on as part of the institutional licence.
- **NHSP could fund DCC adoption.** Libraries applying for NHSP grants in 2026–27 could use DCC as their curriculum platform and pay the licence fee from grant money. DCC should build an "NHSP-ready" pitch page for institutional buyers.
- **No intergenerational model.** Toronto Council on Aging and Connected Canadians both run intergenerational volunteer programmes — a growing funding priority. DCC has no volunteer/mentorship layer, which may limit grant eligibility.
- **Be Connected is the proof of concept at scale.** Australia spent $42M on essentially the same problem. DCC should benchmark against their curriculum scope to demonstrate credibility to Canadian institutional buyers.
